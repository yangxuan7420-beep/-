Move 2 · Introducing the offer

The challenge

Every night, some food is still safe to eat but already moving toward disposal.

Campus supermarkets, bakeries, convenience stores, and small community shops often face the same late-night dilemma. Some food is not expired, its packaging is intact, and its source is clear, yet it becomes hard to sell because the day is ending, demand shifts, or freshness expectations change.

Existing responses such as discounting, returning, reallocating, or discarding may still leave a gap. The real problem is often not only the food itself, but the lack of a fast, traceable, low-burden coordination system.

What we are building

Not a large independent food bank. Not an overpromised AI app. We are crowdfunding a lightweight pilot toolkit that helps trusted local partners register, review, move, label, and publicly log eligible short-dated food.

Food registration

Stores submit food name, quantity, shelf life, storage needs, pickup window, and photo in one standard form.

Eligibility prompts

AI flags whether an item may need cooling, urgent distribution, or manual review under preset rules.

Volunteer routing

Pickup order is suggested from store location, time window, remaining shelf life, and volunteer availability.

Transparent reporting

Each day can end with a clear summary of what was collected, distributed, rejected, and why.

How AI helps

AI handles the coordination layer so people can focus on review, trust, and safe handoff.

Information cleanup

AI turns uneven store submissions into one readable intake table with consistent fields and clearer priorities.

Rule-based prompts

It warns when food may need cooling, quick distribution, or manual checking under the pilot's intake rules.

Route suggestions

It proposes pickup order based on timing, shelf life, volunteer availability, and distribution-point location.

Label drafting

It generates readable labels with source, pickup time, storage notes, suggested consumption window, and reminders.

Daily public logs

It helps draft transparent summaries showing what moved, what did not, and how pilot resources were used.

AI supports the workflow. It does not replace institutional review, volunteer judgment, or food-safety decisions.

Move 3 · Establishing credentials

Why this concept is credible

We are framing the project around realistic boundaries, not heroic promises.

As a student team, our credibility comes from narrowing the promise, defining the process, and building a partner-ready pilot that respects the limits of student-led work.

Bounded pilot design

We focus on a 30-day test in one campus or community setting, with 3-5 partner stores and one distribution point, instead of claiming city-wide coverage.

Human review stays central

AI only assists with organization, reminders, labels, and reporting. Final decisions remain with partner institutions, staff, and trained volunteers.

Safety and traceability first

The pilot accepts only unexpired, sealed, traceable, lower-risk food and records source, pickup time, storage notes, and recommended consumption window.

“We believe social innovation does not have to start with a giant platform. It can begin with a campus, a few willing partners, and one process people can actually trust.”

Move 7 · Describing transactional details

What the pilot includes

A 30-day test with a visible timeline, a bounded budget, and concrete operating tools.

30-day timeline

  1. Week 1 · Prepare Confirm partner stores, set acceptance rules, build forms, and train volunteers.
  2. Weeks 2-3 · Run Log food, arrange pickup, generate labels, distribute eligible items, and publish daily records.
  3. Week 4 · Review Summarize the data, reflect on risks, publish spending, and produce a pilot report.

Where the funds go

Public display and transportation support

Eligible for pilot intake

  • Unexpired food
  • Sealed packaging
  • Traceable source
  • Lower-risk product types
  • Short distribution window still possible

Not accepted

  • Expired food
  • Opened or damaged packaging
  • Unknown origin
  • High-risk prepared meals in phase one
  • Items with unclear storage conditions
Move 8 · Addressing risks and challenges

Risk and boundaries

We take uncertainty seriously, so the project is designed with limits, checks, and public accountability.

Food safety risk

We exclude expired, opened, damaged, unknown-source, and higher-risk food in the first phase.

Unstable store participation

We start with only a few partner locations and reduce the reporting burden through simple forms and templates.

Unstable food supply

We do not promise fixed daily volume. The model uses same-day confirmation plus trend-based planning support.

AI fallibility

AI never makes final safety decisions. It assists sorting, reminders, labels, route suggestions, and reports.

Limited student capacity

The project is positioned as a pilot toolkit that works with partner institutions rather than replacing them.
