At closing time

Good food often disappears without a scene.

A tray of bread is still sealed, but tomorrow's batch will look fresher. A convenience store has bottled drinks left after a rainy day. A campus cafe has packaged snacks after exam week, when foot traffic changed without warning.

At the same time, students return late from the library. Security guards, dormitory staff, cleaners, and night-shift workers finish long shifts. The food is still useful. The missing pieces are timing, information, and responsibility.

Bread bundle Best before tomorrow
Bottled drinks Sealed and traceable

The safety line

Before we rescue food, we decide what not to touch.

Eligible in the first pilot

  • Sealed packaged food
  • Bread, biscuits, cereal, bottled drinks, and instant food
  • Uncut fruit and traceable dairy products still within their shelf life
  • Items with a clear source, intact packaging, and confirmable storage

Left out on purpose

  • Expired food
  • Opened or damaged packages
  • Unknown-source items
  • High-risk prepared meals or unclear cold-chain items

Risks and safeguards

Food rescue earns trust only when the limits are visible.

Risk

Food safety uncertainty

Expired, damaged, opened, unknown-source, or unclear-storage food will not be accepted.

Safeguard: intake rules, label requirements, and human review before any distribution.
Risk

Unstable food supply

Stores may not have eligible food every day, so fixed rescue volumes would be misleading.

Safeguard: same-day confirmation and honest reporting instead of volume promises.
Risk

AI misunderstanding

AI may miss context or produce incomplete suggestions.

Safeguard: AI organizes records; people make the final safety and pickup decisions.
Risk

Student team capacity

The pilot cannot replace a professional food bank, logistics service, or food-safety authority.

Safeguard: one distribution point, 3-5 stores, and a 30-day evaluation boundary.
