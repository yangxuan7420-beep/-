At closing time

Good food often disappears quietly.

Near the end of the evening, campus supermarkets, convenience stores, bakeries, and small cafes sometimes face an awkward shelf: food that is still sealed, traceable, and within its shelf life, but no longer easy to sell before the lights go out.

A loaf looks less fresh than tomorrow's batch. Bottled drinks are left after a rainy day. Packaged snacks sit through exam week, holidays, or a sudden change in foot traffic. Nothing dramatic happens. The item simply moves closer to a discount bin, a write-off record, or the trash.

Nearby, there are people for whom that same food could still matter: students coming back from the library, classmates managing a tight budget, security guards, dormitory staff, cleaners, and night-shift workers. Tonight, Not Wasted exists for that small window between "still safe" and "too late."

The workflow

A simple system for the last few hours of food.

Tonight, Not Wasted is not a student-run food bank, and it is not an AI system making safety decisions. It is a coordination toolkit for stores, school departments, community organizations, charity groups, and volunteers who already want to help but need a cleaner process.

The platform turns scattered messages into a repeatable routine: record the food, check the rules, choose a pickup window, print a clear label, and publish what happened afterward.

01

List the food

Stores enter the name, quantity, shelf life, storage needs, pickup time, source, and photo.

02

Check the rules

AI helps flag refrigeration needs, urgent timing, missing details, and cases that need manual review.

03

Match the pickup

Volunteers see practical suggestions based on location, time window, remaining shelf life, and availability.

04

Label clearly

Each accepted batch carries source, pickup time, storage notes, suggested consumption time, and allergy reminders.

05

Publish the outcome

Supporters can see what was collected, shared, rejected, and how the money was used.

The safety line

Food we can handle carefully

  • Sealed packaged food
  • Bread, biscuits, cereal, bottled drinks, and instant food
  • Uncut fruit and traceable dairy products still within shelf life
  • Food with clear source, intact packaging, and confirmable storage conditions

Food we will leave out

  • Expired food
  • Opened food or damaged packages
  • Unknown-source items
  • High-risk prepared meals or food with unclear storage conditions
Why this can work

The promise is deliberately narrow.

We are a student team, so our credibility does not come from pretending to be a professional food bank, logistics company, or regulatory body. It comes from setting a careful boundary and proving that the process can be followed.

The first pilot will run for 30 days with 3-5 partner stores, one fixed distribution point, trained volunteers, and public records. That small scope is the point: it gives us enough room to learn without asking the community to trust a system that has not yet earned it.

People decide

AI organizes information, drafts labels, suggests routes, and prepares reports. Trained people make final calls.

Safety beats volume

We will not promise a fixed daily amount. We will only handle food that meets the pilot's intake rules.

Trust needs records

Every accepted, distributed, or rejected batch should leave a visible record for partners and supporters.

Stay in the loop

This should be a visible community experiment, not a one-way act of charity.

Stores can join as pilot partners. Volunteers can help with communication, pickup, labeling, sorting, and distribution records. Supporters can follow public updates, ask questions, and help us improve the model while the pilot is still running.

During the 30 days, we plan to share partner progress, accepted food categories, distribution results, rejected items, volunteer participation, and fund use. At the end, we will publish a review of what worked, what failed, and what should change before any future expansion.

The 30-day plan

Small enough to manage. Long enough to learn.

We will not promise a fixed amount of redistributed food, because short-dated supply is uncertain. We will promise a clear routine, cautious intake, honest records, and a final report the community can read.

  1. Week 1

    Set the boundary

    Contact 3-5 potential partner stores, confirm one distribution point, write intake rules, prepare online forms, create label templates, and train volunteers.

  2. Weeks 2-3

    Run the routine

    Stores submit eligible food. AI organizes records, drafts labels, and suggests pickup priorities. Volunteers and partner staff review, label, distribute, and record the results.

  3. Week 4

    Show what happened

    Summarize partner participation, food categories, accepted and rejected items, volunteer activities, fund use, and operational problems in a final pilot report.

Designed with limits

Food rescue only deserves trust when the risks are visible.

The pilot will be useful only if it is honest about what it can and cannot do. We want the limits to be part of the design, not a footnote.

Food safety

We will not accept expired food, opened food, damaged packages, unknown-source food, or items with unclear storage conditions.

Store participation

Stores may not have eligible food every day. We will start with 3-5 partners and keep the intake process simple.

Food supply

We will use same-day confirmation instead of claiming precise prediction. Historical data only supports trend analysis and scheduling.

AI assistance

AI may misunderstand information, so it will never make final safety decisions. Human review remains essential.

Student capacity

We are not operating a complete food bank. This is a pilot toolkit for schools, communities, stores, or charity organizations to test carefully.

Waste is not always caused by lack of care.
