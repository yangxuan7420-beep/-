The Problem

Food waste can begin at closing time, quietly and routinely.

When campus supermarkets, convenience stores, bakeries, and small cafes are about to close, some food reaches an awkward moment. It is not expired. Its package is still complete. Its source is clear. Yet because it looks less fresh than tomorrow's stock, or because demand changed with the weather, exams, holidays, or closing time, it may quietly move toward discount shelves, write-off records, or disposal.

At the same time, there are people nearby who could use a timely and dignified meal or snack: students returning late from the library, classmates living on a tight budget, security guards, dormitory staff, cleaners, and night-shift workers.

Tonight, Not Wasted is an AI-assisted coordination platform designed to help still-edible short-dated food reach people who need it, through better information, clearer labels, trained volunteers, and transparent reporting.

Move 2 · Introducing the Offer

A lightweight collaboration toolkit, not a student-run food bank.

Tonight, Not Wasted is designed for existing or potential local partners: stores, community organizations, school departments, charity groups, and volunteers. It does not replace professional food banks, and it does not let AI decide whether food is safe.

Instead, it helps people coordinate the steps that are often slow, unclear, or too dependent on temporary chat messages.

01

Register

Stores submit food name, quantity, shelf life, storage requirements, pickup time, source, and photo.

02

Prompt

AI gives rule-based intake prompts, such as refrigeration, urgent distribution, or manual review.

03

Route

Volunteers receive pickup suggestions based on location, time window, shelf life, and availability.

Labels show source, pickup time, storage notes, suggested consumption time, and allergy reminders.

05

Report

Public summaries show what was collected, distributed, rejected, and how funds were used.

Safety Boundary

What enters the pilot

  • Sealed packaged food
  • Bread, biscuits, cereal, bottled drinks, and instant food
  • Uncut fruit and traceable dairy products still within shelf life
  • Food with clear source, intact packaging, and confirmable storage conditions

What does not enter the pilot

  • Expired food
  • Opened food or damaged packages
  • Unknown-source items
  • High-risk prepared meals or food with unclear storage conditions
Move 3 · Establishing Credentials

Our credibility comes from a narrow promise and a clear process.

As a student team, our strength is not that we can replace professional food banks, logistics systems, or regulatory institutions. Our strength is that we can design a careful pilot, define clear boundaries, and use AI to reduce the coordination burden in a realistic setting.

We have deliberately narrowed the project to a 30-day trial with 3-5 partner stores, one fixed distribution point, trained volunteers, and transparent public records. This limited scope makes the project more responsible and easier to evaluate.

Human review first

AI assists with organizing information, drafting labels, suggesting routes, and generating reports.

Safety before quantity

We do not promise a fixed daily amount. We only handle food that meets the pilot's intake rules.

Transparency builds trust

Each accepted, distributed, or rejected batch should leave a visible record for supporters and partners.

Move 6 · Encouraging Further Online Communication

This should feel like a transparent community experiment, not a one-way act of charity.

Stores can join as pilot partners and submit eligible short-dated food through a simple intake process. Volunteers can help with communication, pickup, labeling, sorting, and distribution records. Supporters can follow our public updates and help us improve the model by asking questions and giving feedback.

During the pilot, we plan to publish regular updates about partner stores, accepted food categories, distribution results, rejected items, volunteer participation, and fund use. At the end of the 30 days, we will release a pilot review explaining what worked, what did not work, and what should change before any future expansion.

Move 7 · Describing Transactional Details

The pilot runs for 30 days in three stages.

We will not promise a fixed amount of redistributed food, because supply is uncertain. Instead, we promise to make the process clear, cautious, and publicly reviewable.

  1. Week 1

    Preparation

    Contact 3-5 potential partner stores, confirm one distribution point, design intake rules, prepare online forms, create label templates, and train volunteers.

  2. Weeks 2-3

    Operation

    Stores submit eligible food information. AI organizes records, drafts labels, and suggests pickup priorities. Volunteers and partner staff review, label, distribute, and record results.

  3. Week 4

    Review

    Summarize partner participation, food categories, accepted and rejected items, volunteer activities, fund use, and operational problems in a final pilot report.

Move 8 · Addressing Risks and Challenges

Food redistribution involves real uncertainty, so the pilot is designed with limits.

We do not want to hide the risks. We want to make them visible, manageable, and reviewable.

Food safety risk

We will not accept expired food, opened food, damaged packages, unknown-source food, or items with unclear storage conditions.

Unstable store participation

Stores may not have eligible food every day. We will start with only 3-5 partners and keep the intake process simple.

Unstable food supply

We will use same-day confirmation instead of claiming precise prediction. Historical data only supports trend analysis and scheduling.

AI limitations

AI may misunderstand information, so it will never make final safety decisions. Human review remains essential.

Limited student capacity

We are not claiming to operate a complete food bank. This is a pilot toolkit that should work with schools, communities, stores, or charity organizations.

Waste is not always caused by lack of care.

Sometimes it is caused by missing information, unclear responsibility, and poor timing. With your support, we can test whether AI-assisted coordination can help still-edible food move from ordinary shelves to ordinary people, before another ordinary night ends in waste.
