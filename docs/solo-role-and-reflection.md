# Solo Role Breakdown and Reflection

## Solo Role Breakdown

This repository is intentionally documented as a solo project. The work is divided by responsibility rather than by team member.

| Responsibility | What was done |
| --- | --- |
| Project framing | Chose the product concept, decided on `方案 C`, and defined the final delivery scope |
| Content writing | Drafted and revised the crowdfunding copy in English |
| Web production | Organized files and built the static page using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript |
| AI process documentation | Recorded tool usage, prompt iterations, limitations, and transparency notes |
| Submission preparation | Prepared README, checklist, and supporting materials for final course submission |

## Reflection (Long Version)

Working on this project made me see AI less as a shortcut and more as a collaborator that still needs careful supervision. AI was most helpful when I needed to move from a vague idea to a workable structure. It helped me break down the assignment, shape the crowdfunding page into clear rhetorical sections, and turn abstract product ideas into usable copy more quickly than I could have done alone from a blank page. In that sense, AI clearly saved time during the early stages of planning and drafting.

However, AI also created a different kind of workload. It often produced language that sounded polished and persuasive, but not always realistic for the context of a student assignment. Because of that, I had to spend time checking whether the content was honest, whether the claims felt credible, and whether the final page still reflected my own judgment. The more fluent the output was, the more important it became to slow down and edit carefully. AI was helpful for generating options, but it was weak at understanding when a project risked becoming exaggerated or too ambitious.

The most important human role in this process was making decisions about scope and truthfulness. I had to decide what to keep, what to simplify, and what not to pretend had already been finished. That was especially important in the sections about visuals, evidence, and product credibility. AI could help me word those sections, but it could not take responsibility for their accuracy.

Overall, I would use AI again in similar projects, and probably even more often, but with clearer boundaries. I now see its value most strongly in drafting, restructuring, and iteration. I would not rely on it to replace final judgment, project ownership, or ethical responsibility.
