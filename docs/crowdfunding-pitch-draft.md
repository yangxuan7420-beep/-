# Tonight, Not Wasted

## AI-Assisted Short-Dated Food Redistribution Platform

### Move 1: Gaining Attention

Every evening, when campus supermarkets, convenience stores, bakeries, and small cafes are about to close, some food reaches an awkward moment. It is not expired. Its package is still complete. Its source is clear. Yet because it looks less fresh than tomorrow's stock, or because demand changed with the weather, exams, holidays, or closing time, it may quietly move toward discount shelves, write-off records, or disposal.

At the same time, there are people nearby who could use a timely and dignified meal or snack: students returning late from the library, classmates living on a tight budget, security guards, dormitory staff, cleaners, and night-shift workers.

**Tonight, Not Wasted** is an AI-assisted coordination platform designed to help still-edible short-dated food travel one step further before it is thrown away. We are crowdfunding a 30-day campus or community pilot to test whether better information, clearer labels, trained volunteers, and transparent reporting can make small-scale food redistribution safer, faster, and more trustworthy.

### Move 2: Introducing the Offer

Tonight, Not Wasted is not a student-run food bank, and it is not a fully automated AI system that decides whether food is safe. Instead, it is a lightweight collaboration toolkit for existing or potential local partners: stores, community organizations, school departments, charity groups, and volunteers.

The platform helps partners complete five practical tasks:

- Register food information in a standard form, including name, quantity, shelf life, storage requirements, pickup time, source store, and photo.
- Give rule-based intake prompts, such as whether an item needs refrigeration, urgent distribution, or manual review.
- Suggest volunteer pickup routes based on store location, pickup window, remaining shelf life, volunteer availability, and distribution point.
- Generate clear labels with source, pickup time, storage notes, suggested consumption time, and allergy reminders.
- Produce transparent daily or weekly reports showing what was collected, what was distributed, what was rejected, and how funds were used.

The first pilot will focus on lower-risk items such as sealed packaged food, bread, biscuits, cereal, bottled drinks, instant food, uncut fruit, and traceable dairy products that are still within their shelf life. We will not accept expired food, opened food, damaged packages, unknown-source food, or high-risk prepared meals in the first phase.

Our goal is modest but meaningful: to create a repeatable workflow that helps a small campus or community reduce avoidable food waste without shifting safety responsibility onto recipients.

### Move 3: Establishing Credentials

As a student team, our strength is not that we can replace professional food banks, logistics systems, or regulatory institutions. Our strength is that we can design a careful pilot, define clear boundaries, and use AI to reduce the coordination burden in a realistic setting.

We have deliberately narrowed the project to a 30-day trial with 3-5 partner stores, one fixed distribution point, trained volunteers, and transparent public records. This limited scope makes the project more responsible and easier to evaluate.

The project is built on three principles:

- **Human review first:** AI only assists with organizing information, drafting labels, suggesting routes, and generating reports. Final decisions must be made by trained people and partner organizations.
- **Safety before quantity:** We do not promise to redistribute a fixed amount of food every day. We only handle food that meets the pilot's intake rules.
- **Transparency builds trust:** Every accepted, distributed, or rejected batch should leave a visible record, so supporters can see both the results and the limits of the pilot.

We believe social innovation does not have to begin with a large platform or a heroic promise. It can begin with one campus, a few responsible partners, and a process people can actually trust.

### Move 6: Encouraging Further Online Communication

Tonight, Not Wasted depends on cooperation. We welcome three kinds of participation:

- Volunteers can help with communication, pickup, labeling, sorting, and distribution records.
- Supporters can follow our public updates and help us improve the model by asking questions and giving feedback.

We do not want this project to feel like a one-way act of charity. We want it to become a transparent community experiment in which stores, volunteers, recipients, and supporters all understand how the process works.

### Move 7: Describing Transactional Details

The pilot will run for 30 days and will be divided into three stages.

**Week 1: Preparation**

We will contact 3-5 potential partner stores, confirm one distribution point, design the food intake rules, prepare online forms and inventory sheets, create label templates, and train volunteers. This stage is about setting boundaries before any food enters the system.

**Weeks 2-3: Operation**

Stores will submit eligible food information through the intake form. AI will organize the information, draft labels, and suggest pickup priorities. Volunteers and partner staff will review the items, complete pickup, label accepted food, distribute it at the designated point, and record the results.

**Week 4: Review**

We will summarize the data, including partner participation, food categories, accepted and rejected items, volunteer activities, fund use, and operational problems. We will publish a final pilot report and decide whether the model is suitable for future improvement or replication.

The pilot has several planned goals:

- Connect 3-5 stores or food sources.
- Establish one fixed distribution point.
- Build one standard food intake and labeling process.
- Test one AI-assisted inventory and reporting workflow.
- Identify which food categories are suitable for low-risk redistribution.
- Publish a transparent report on results, limits, and fund use.

We will not promise a fixed amount of redistributed food, because food supply is uncertain. Instead, we promise to make the process clear, cautious, and publicly reviewable.

### Move 8: Addressing Risks and Challenges

Food redistribution involves real uncertainty, and we do not want to hide that. The pilot faces several risks.

**Food safety risk:** We will not accept expired food, opened food, damaged packages, unknown-source food, or items with unclear storage conditions. The first stage will avoid high-risk prepared meals and difficult cold-chain products. Accepted items will be labeled with source, pickup time, storage notes, and suggested consumption time.

**Unstable store participation:** Stores may not have eligible food every day, and some may not have time to join. We will start with only 3-5 partners and keep the intake process simple to reduce their workload.

**Unstable food supply:** Short-dated food cannot be guaranteed in fixed amounts. We will use same-day confirmation rather than claiming precise prediction. Historical data will be used only to identify trends, improve volunteer scheduling, and reduce empty trips.

**AI limitations:** AI may misunderstand information or produce incomplete suggestions. For this reason, AI will never make final safety decisions. It will only assist with information organization, prompts, route suggestions, label drafting, and report generation. Human review remains essential.

**Limited student capacity:** We are not claiming to operate a complete food bank. This project is a pilot toolkit that should work with schools, communities, stores, or charity organizations. If the pilot reveals problems, we will record them honestly instead of treating them as failure.

Tonight, Not Wasted begins with a simple belief: waste is not always caused by lack of care. Sometimes it is caused by missing information, unclear responsibility, and poor timing. With your support, we can test whether AI-assisted coordination can help still-edible food move from ordinary shelves to ordinary people, before another ordinary night ends in waste.
